## About Page Content Types

### One-Liner / Hero Statement
- Single, punchy sentence at the top of About page
- Captures essence of who you are and what you do
- Pattern: "[What you do] who [specialization/focus] to [impact/outcome]"
- Example: "A product designer who transforms complex healthcare systems into intuitive, human-centered experiences"

### Short Bio (2-3 paragraphs)
- Professional overview
- Key expertise and domains
- What makes your approach unique
- Current focus or availability

### Full Bio (4-5 paragraphs)
- Journey into design
- Key experiences and evolution
- Design philosophy and approach
- Expertise and specializations
- Personal touch (interests, values)
- Call to action

### Skills/Capabilities Section
- Not just a list, but context-rich descriptions
- Pattern: "Capability: Brief explanation of how you apply it"

## Best Practices for About Page Content

### What Makes Strong About Content:

**Authentic voice** - Sounds like a real person, not a resume
**Specific examples** - "healthcare and fintech" not "various industries"
**Outcome-focused** - What you enable/achieve, not just what you do
**Strategic word choice** - Keywords integrated naturally
**Balanced tone** - Professional but personable

### Effective Opening Lines:

- "I'm a [role] who [unique approach] to [impact]"
- "I design [what] for [who], focusing on [differentiator]"
- "My work sits at the intersection of [X] and [Y]"
- Start with impact: "I've spent X years helping [stakeholders] achieve [outcomes]"

### Strong Middle Content:

- Journey: "I came to design through [unconventional path/background]"
- Philosophy: "I believe great design is [your perspective]"
- Expertise: "I specialize in [domains], particularly [specific challenge]"
- Approach: "My process combines [methods] with [unique element]"

### Compelling Closings:

- Current availability/interests
- Call to action (connect, collaborate)
- Personal touch (when appropriate)
- Forward-looking statement

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid:

- Generic buzzwords: "passionate," "innovative," "thought leader"
- Resume language: "Responsible for," "Managed team of"
- Vague claims: "I create great user experiences"
- Too formal: "Mr./Ms. [Name] is a professional..."
- Too casual: "Hey there! I'm a designer who loves coffee and pixels"
- Too long: More than 5 paragraphs becomes tedious
